<div class="row page-article-image-group"> <div class="col-xs-12"> <div class="rich_text_area-style component-gap"> <div class="row"> <div class="col-xs-10 col-sm-10 variable-text-offset-1"> <p class="inspire-subhead">If you don’t care about quality or karma, free images are everywhere. But you do care, don’t you? At the very least, you don’t want to grab an image from the Internet, use it for a job, and risk your client being slapped with a lawsuit for copyright infringement. High-quality, free images that you can legally modify for commercial projects <i>do</i> exist. Here’s where to find digital files you can use in any <a shape="rect" href="https://www.adobe.com/creativecloud/" target="_blank">Creative Cloud</a> application.</p>
<p class="inspire-subhead">LEADING THE WAY: RIJKS MUSEUM</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">Start by visiting the website of the <a shape="rect" href="https://www.rijksmuseum.nl/" target="_blank">Rijks Museum</a>. In late 2012, the museum began a bold experiment by not only making it possible to download high-quality files of its collection, but by actively encouraging people to do so.</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">The Rijks’ Linda Volkers explains: “By allowing people to use and remix our collection, our art gets new relevance in today’s world. People we call ‘culture snackers’ don’t necessarily go to museums, but they like beautiful things. When they see and enjoy our art online, they are connected to it even though they don’t visit in person. It pulls down barriers. You don’t have to know everything about this piece of art. If you look at it and have a nicer day and are inspired to do something with the art, it’s good for the people and for us.”</p> </div> </div> </div> </div>

(“In 2017, our top 10 entries were from 9 countries,” says Volkers.)</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">But let’s get back to what you really care about—the freebies. To get an image, browse the website, and when you see something you like, simply click the scissor icon to download it. The only requirement is that you first register for a free account called a <a shape="rect" href="https://www.rijksmuseum.nl/en/rijksstudio" target="_blank">Rijksstudio</a>. You can browse by artist, style, subject matter, and more. Most of the collection is no longer under copyright, but if you stumble across the odd outlier, you’ll see a “not available to download” message.</p>
<p class="inspire-subhead">NATIONAL GALLERY OF ART</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">How about more than <a shape="rect" href="https://images.nga.gov/en/page/show_home_page.html" target="_blank">50,000 downloadable images</a> that are OK to use for commercial purposes? That’s what the United States’ National Gallery of Art offers. Although some newer works are still under copyright, the distinction is clear—you can’t download copyrighted images, only those in the public domain. </p> </div> </div> </div> </div>

<p class="inspire-subhead">GETTY MUSEUM</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">There are millions of assets on the Getty Museum website, but only 114,000 are in the public domain. Fortunately, there’s an easy way to zero in on the copyright-free files: On the <a shape="rect" href="http://search.getty.edu/gateway/search?q=&amp;cat=highlight&amp;f=%22Open+Content+Images%22&amp;rows=10&amp;srt=a&amp;dir=s&amp;pg=1" target="_blank">Search page</a>, make sure that you filter by Open Content Images . From there, you’ll find many ways to further narrow your search results; for example, by topic, location, and type. Fans of Italian art and architecture will be especially pleased by the sizeable Foto Arte Minore archive. All Open Content assets are at least 300 ppi. </p> </div> </div> </div> </div>

<p class="inspire-body-text">You’ll find thousands of high-resolution, downloadable images on the <a shape="rect" href="http://art.thewalters.org/" target="_blank">Walters Art Museum website</a>. There are several ways to browse the collection (by category, place, medium, and more). You can save what you like in collections or download as you go. The museum has assigned the <a shape="rect" href="https://creativecommons.org/publicdomain/zero/1.0/" target="_blank">Creative Commons Zero: No Rights Reserved</a> license to all of these images, which allows you to copy, modify, and distribute them, even for commercial purposes. </p> </div> </div> </div> </div>

<div class="row page-article-image-group"> <div class="col-xs-12"> <div class="rich_text_area_1650160400-style component-gap"> <div class="row"> <div class="col-xs-10 col-sm-10 variable-text-offset-1"> <p class="inspire-subhead">NASA and NOAA</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">You may already know about NASA’s extensive (140,000 files and growing!) <a shape="rect" href="https://images.nasa.gov/" target="_blank">image and video library</a>. It’s a great resource for imagery related to aeronautics, astrophysics, Earth science, and, of course, spaceflight. Many of the images and most of the videos are in the public domain and therefore OK for commercial use. Do be careful to avoid assets with the NASA logo and astronauts or NASA employees; see the <a shape="rect" href="https://www.nasa.gov/audience/formedia/features/Advertising_Guidelines.html" target="_blank">advertising guidelines</a> for more information.</p>
<p class="inspire-body-text">For more of the natural (and, occasionally, unnatural) world, from recent weather events to old drawings of animals, try the <a shape="rect" href="http://www.photolib.noaa.gov/index.html" target="_blank">image library of the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA). Most of its 32,000+ images are in the public domain. Artistic merit can be spotty (the scientists understandably prioritize their work, not the photos), and the website’s organization is clunky. However, you’ll be rewarded for digging deeper into this collection; for example, visit the “<a shape="rect" href="http://www.photolib.noaa.gov/library/index.html" target="_blank">Treasures of the Library</a>" section<b> </b>for images from rare assets, such as a 1695 book that shows us what a Baltic Sea merman looked like. </p> </div> </div> </div> </div>
